From Booting Up to Building Empires

I wasn't a natural in the academic world, especially when it came to the theoretical side of BBIT (Bachelor of Business and Information Technology) program. Growing up, I was more hands-on, taking apart electronics and tinkering with software, but formal education often felt like a chore. My early academic experiences in the BBIT program were marked by:
1. Struggling with theory:
Concepts like database management, networking protocols, and business strategy felt abstract and disconnected from the practical application, I craved.
2. Difficulty focusing in lectures:
Long lectures filled with jargon and complex diagrams often left me feeling lost and overwhelmed.
3. Procrastination:
Feeling discouraged, I would often put off studying until the last minute, leading to rushed work and poor grades.

My first year was particularly tough. I barely passed some core modules and started to doubt if I was cut out for the program. However, a few key events shifted my perspective:
1. Discovering coding:
In a programming elective, something clicked. I found the logic of coding intuitive and enjoyed the immediate feedback of seeing my code come to life.
2. Finding a mentor:
A senior BBIT student, noticed my struggles and offered to mentor me. She helped me connect the theoretical concepts to real-world applications and introduced me to online resources and coding communities.
3. Participating in a hackathon:
She encouraged me to join a local hackathon. The pressure of building a working prototype in a short time forced me to apply the knowledge practically, solidifying my understanding and boosting my confidence.

This experiences ignited a passion in me. I started spending more time in the computer lab, experimenting with different programming languages and building small projects. I found that by applying the theoretical knowledge in practical ways, it became much easier to understand and retain. Despite the initial setbacks, I persevered.

Some of the challenges I faced were:
1. Balancing coursework with personal projects: Juggling academic assignments with my growing interest in personal coding projects required careful time management.
2. Dealing with imposter syndrome:
Even as my skills improved, sometimes I felt like I didn't belong in the tech world.
3. Navigating the job market:
Finding internships and entry-level positions required networking and building a strong portfolio.
But I learned to leverage my strengths. I focused on developing a strong portfolio of projects, actively participated in online forums, and networked with professionals in the field.

Years later, I'm not just a BBIT undergraduate; I'm a successful software developer leading a team at a thriving tech startup. I had turned my initial struggles into a strength, understanding the importance of both theory and practice.
My journey, from a struggling BBIT student to a respected tech professional, proved that with passion, mentorship, and a willingness to learn, anyone can overcome academic challenges and build a successful career in the tech industry
